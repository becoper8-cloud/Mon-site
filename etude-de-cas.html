Wilde Aparthotels London – Covent Garden est un établissement au cœur de Londresproposant une expérience hybride entre le confort d’un appartement et lesprestations d’un hôtel haut de gamme. Intervention stratégique

La première étape a consisté à reprendre en main la fiche Google Business Profile de l’établissement. Becomes Perfect a revu l’ensemble des éléments devisibilité : description optimisée pour le SEO local, mise en avant des atoutsspécifiques à Wilde (emplacement, design, équipements), ajout de visuelsimmersifs professionnels, et publication hebdomadaire de contenus viaGoogle Posts.En parallèle, une stratégie de sollicitation d’avis authentiques a été miseen place. Côté conversion, un audit UX du site web a permis de repérer plusieurszones de friction : navigation peu intuitive sur mobile, appels à l’action peuvisibles, et absence d’avis clients intégrés dans les étapes clés du tunnel deréservation. Le taux de conversion en ligne a progressé de manière notable. Il y a beaucoup d’inertie dans l’expérience d’achat de produits de soins pour hommes : les

Fragmentation des récompenses
‍
Un autre levier que nos recherches ont indiqué comme étant potentiellement fructueux était
la valeur perçue : si nous pouvions trouver un moyen d’augmenter la valeur perçue du

marques et leurs habitudes d’achat.
‍
Lors de notre analyse heuristique du tunnel d'abonnement, nous avons constaté que la
plupart des produits étaient présentés sous forme d'offre groupée.
> Si cette présentation était esthétiquement réussie (les images étaient agréables), elle
contrevenait à un principe bien connu du design comportemental : Coûts forfaitaires et
récompenses fragmentaires.
‍
En regroupant les coûts, vous pouvez minimiser leur importance perçue. En fragmentant les
récompenses, vous augmentez l'attention portée à chacune, amplifiant leur valeur perçue.

Nous avons donc décidé de repenser certains éléments de la page : plus précisément, nous

> Résultat : Cette variante a augmenté le nombre d'abonnements de 11,2 % par rapport à
la version d'origine.Itérer pour obtenir un impact

Puis une autre variante où certaines options étaient désélectionnées par défaut.

> La première variante a augmenté le taux de conversion de 1,5 %
> La deuxième variante, avec les cases désélectionnées, a augmenté les abonnements de
6,8 % supplémentaires.

Soit un gain total de +17 % en conversion avec ces deux tests cumulés.

A surprise insight leads to big lift for Mark's
Work WearhouseClient: Mark's Work Wearhouse

Opportunity
‍
Mark's Work Wearhouse is one of Canada’s top retailers for workwear, footwear, and casual clothing. Their
mission is to outfit Canadians with reliable, durable apparel for work and everyday life. We partnered with
Mark's Work Wearhouse just over a year ago and have been working together to optimize their
e-commerce experiences, with the goal of increasing conversions in the form of transactions.


A Bit of Context
‍
While Mark's Work Wearhouse’s conversion optimization program is multi-faceted, two different tests
recently revealed impactful insights about one of the company’s value propositions.
A value proposition can be thought of as a cost-versus-benefits equation that reveals your prospects’
motivation. But it’s all about perception: if your perceived benefits outweigh the perceived costs, your
prospects will be motivated to act.
Motivation = Perceived Benefits - Perceived Costs
All value propositions have varying degrees of value depending on how they’re interpreted (by the
prospect) and how they’re communicated (by the marketer). Your benefits hold different weight for different
people—it’s all about identifying which benefits are perceived to be most important.
Users are unique, and tiny changes can have massive effects when your value proposition sits on the fine
line between useless and valuable. Often, the same value proposition can have dramatically different
effects when framed in slightly different ways.
The following results demonstrate just how powerful value proposition discoveries can be.


Solution
‍
The Cart Page Test
‍
The Necessity of ‘Free Shipping’
‍
Mark's Work Wearhouse offers free shipping on online orders over a certain dollar amount. In today’s
e-commerce landscape, some form of free shipping is expected—it’s a Point of Parity, a baseline feature
customers assume most retailers provide.
The key question was: How can Mark's Work Wearhouse communicate this offer in a way that gives it more
perceived value? How can they transform a Point of Parity into a Point of Difference—a feature that’s not
only important but uniquely delivered by their brand?
At the time of the test, Mark's Work Wearhouse offered free shipping on all orders over $25.


The Hypothesis
‍
On their original cart page, they used a standard message: “Free shipping on all orders of $25 or more.”
With an average order value over $100, most users already qualified for free shipping.
Instead of simply stating the fact (a generic message designed for all possibilities), the idea was to optimize
for probability—speak directly to what most customers are experiencing.
The hypothesis: Increasing the visibility of the 'Free Shipping' benefit and explicitly congratulating
users who qualify will improve motivation and boost conversions.
Rather than requiring users to figure it out, the new messaging told them:
- “Congratulations, you qualify for free shipping!”
- For those just under the threshold: “Just $17.45 more to qualify for free shipping.”

As usability expert Steve Krug says, “Don’t make your users think!” Mark's Work Wearhouse turned a passive statement into a moment of celebration.
Note: This test included two variations. Variation A reduced visual distractions and improved
clarity. Variation B built on this by highlighting the “Free Shipping” value proposition.
The final results include both variations.

The Results
The test confirmed the hypothesis. The winning variation led to a 7.3% lift in transactions, with 99.5%
confidence.
For a high-traffic site like Mark's Work Wearhouse, that’s a significant win.

The Product Page Test (Plus, a Bonus Insight!)
‍
Following the success on the cart page, both Mark's Work Wearhouse and our strategy team at Becomes
Perfect wanted to test whether this same messaging could work on the product detail pages.Originally, free
shipping wasn’t mentioned anywhere on these pages—it only appeared in a site-wide banner that many
users closed immediately.
‍

Experiment Setup

- Variation A: Reduced distractions and clarified the call to action.
- Variation B: Built on A and added prominent messaging: “FREE SHIPPING on orders
over $50”.
The Initial Results
‍
Variation B showed promise: a 2.49% lift, but with only 55% confidence—not enough to declare it a clear win.
However, Michael St Laurent noticed that Variation B consistently showed a 6% lift—until the final days of the test.
What changed?
‍

The Turning Point: July 29
‍
On July 29, the free shipping threshold was changed from $50 to $75.
When we reanalyzed the data with this in mind, the findings were striking:
When shipping was free on orders over $50, Variation B delivered a 6.56% lift in conversions
(96% confidence).
When the threshold moved to $75, the same variation led to a 15% decrease in transactions
(94% confidence).
Takeaways for Mark's Work Wearhouse
‍
At $50 (or $25), free shipping is a strong motivator. It should be highlighted prominently.LaAt $75, it may be better left in the background to avoid discouraging shoppers.
‍

Final Analysis
‍
For Mark's Work Wearhouse, free shipping is a highly elastic value proposition. Its perceived value
varies greatly depending on the price threshold.
These experiments revealed that the offer wasn’t being used to its full potential. Now, it’s not only more
visible—it’s strategically framed to maximize impact.
The data is now helping inform larger business decisions, such as where to set future shipping
thresholds for optimal performance.
